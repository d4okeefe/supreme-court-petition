Open Section Note

Many filers like to include main sections that are not required, like an Introduction or some other  main section to help the reader better understand the argument.

Opinions Below Rule

Rule 14.1(d)

OB Plain Language

In this required section, you should list the final orders or opinions of the courts below, and for each such order, cite to the official reporter (or state that the order or opinion was not published), and give the appendix page number where it can be found.

OB Tip

The information provided here is similar to the Statement of Related Cases requirement in the index (Rule 14.1(b)(iii)), but you must include both sections.

OB Tip

The best practice for drafting an OB section is to cite the appendix page number where the justices can find the order or opinion, and you cannot know the correct page reference unless you have completed your appendix.

Jurisdictional Statement Rule

Rule 14.1(e)

JS Plain Language

In this required section you should provide specific information that confirms that the Supreme Court has jurisdiction to hear your petition. This section should be concise and formulaic, simply stating the date of the last order that considered the merits of your case, and if applicable, the date of any order denying rehearing or discretionary review, and any order extending the time to file your petition. Finally, you should cite the specific federal statute that confers jurisdiction on the U.S. Supreme Court.

JS: ATTENTION

In certain unusual types of appeals, Rule 14.1(e) describes other material that should be provided in the JS. We recommend that you read the rule to confirm that those circumstances do not apply to your petition.

JS Form - Federal

A typical JS for a case arising out of the federal courts might read as follows:

The Court of Appeals entered its opinion on ________ and denied the petition for rehearing on ________. On ________, Justice ________ granted a filing extension to ________. This Court may exercise jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1254(1).

JS Form - State Highest Court

A typical JS for a case arising out of a state supreme court might read as follows: The ________ Supreme Court entered its opinion on ________ and denied an application for rehearing on ________. On ________, Justice ________ granted a filing extension to ________. This Court may exercise jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1257(a).

JS Form - State Intermediate Court

A typical JS for a case arising out of an intermediate state appellate court might read as follows:

The ________ Court of Appeals entered its opinion on ________. The ________ Supreme Court denied an application for discretionary review on ________ and denied an application for rehearing on ________. On ________, Justice ________ granted a filing extension to ________. This Court may exercise jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1257(a).

JS Tip

If you are NOT coming from a federal court of appeals (28 U.S.C. § 1254(1)) or a state court (28 U.S.C. § 1257(a)), or if you are NOT requesting a writ of certiorari, you will need to determine the correct federal jurisdictional statute. Petitions for other types of writs (see Rule 20), or that arise out of other courts - territorial courts, etc. - are founded on different jurisdictional statutes.

Constitutional And Statutory Provisions Involved Rule

Rule 14.1(f)

C&S Plain Language

This is a required section where you cite the core constitutional or statutory provision that lies at the heart of the controversy, followed by the actual relevant text of the provision.

C&S Form

You can provide just an excerpt of the relevant provision, even if just a few words. Example: U.S. Const. amend. V, “No person shall ... be deprived of ... property, without due process of law”

C&S Tip

The C&S verbatim text does not count against the word limit, even if presented in the brief.

C&S Tip

If the provisions are lengthy, you can opt to provide a citation to the provisions in the main brief, and then give the verbatim text in the appendix (citing the appropriate appendix page number in the brief).

Statement of the Case Rule

Rule 14.1(g)

SOC Plain Language

In this required section, you should provide a brief recitation of the relevant factual background of the case, and a description of the decisions below.

SOC: ATTENTION

The SOC must make specific disclosures about the federal question, and how it has been preserved for consideration by the U.S. Supreme Court. We strongly recommend that you review the Rule 14.1(g)(i) for cases arising out of state courts and Rule 14.1(g)(ii) for cases arising out of federal courts.

SOC Tip

The SOC is where you describe what did happen, not what should have happened. You can present your SOC in a way that suggests your feelings about what has occurred in this case, but your real persuasion should wait until the next section. Brevity and lucidity are essential to effective advocacy in your SOC.

Argument Rule

Rule 14.1(h)

Argument Plain Language

The heart of your petition, the Argument (sometimes called, “Reasons for Granting the Petition”), is where you explain why the decision below is incorrect, and why the justices should grant the petition so that they can make a thorough examination on the merits stage.

Argument Tip

Rule 10 lays out some scenarios that might prompt the Court to grant a petition:

  1. the decision below conflicts with other courts on “an important federal question;
  2. the lower court decided “an important question of federal law” in a way that conflicts with a Supreme Court decision;
  3. the court below “decided an important question of federal law that has not been, but should be, settled” by the Supreme Court; or
  4. the lower court “has so far departed from the accepted and usual course of proceedings” as to require the Court’s “supervisory power."

A well-crafted Argument should describe how the error below has implications beyond your case. The Supreme Court is not likely to grant your petition only because the error below is unjust.

Conclusion Note

Conclusions are universally expected in legal writing, though for a petition they are not required by rule. We strongly recommend that you include a Conclusion to demonstrate a serious and respectful submission to the Court.

Conclusion Form

The conclusion can be succinct like, “For the reasons stated herein, the petition should be granted,” or you can opt for a slightly more expressive paragraph that briefly summarizes the points made in the Argument.

End Signature Rule

Rule 34.3

Signature Plain Language

End the brief with the name or names of the filers.

Signature Form

Above the signature, you should type a courteous, “Respectfully submitted,” leading into a signature block with the filers’ names and other information. Many filers simply cut-and-paste the filer information block text from the cover.

Signature Form - IFP (Rule 34.2)

Include a blank line above the signature block, and far enough below the, “Respectfully submitted,” line to accommodate your signature.

Signature Form - Booklet (Rule 34.1)

You do not actually sign your booklet filing, so you should not include a signature line, just the printed filer information.
