Index

All text after the Cover and through the Table of Authorities comprise the Index.

  • The text in the Index does not count towards the word limit. Rule 33.1(d)
  • Index pages are numbered consecutively with small Roman numerals (romanettes).
Questions Presented Rule

Rule 14.1(a)

Questions Presented Plain Language

In this required section, you should provide a short and concise presentation of the legal controversy that you are asking the Court to resolve with your petition, without getting too deeply into the specific circumstances of your case.

Questions Presented: ATTENTION

The Clerk’s Office might reject your petition if you include too much text before your first question. A short introductory statement is permitted, but Rule 14.1(a) says that the Questions Presented should “be set out on the first page following the cover,” and the Clerk’s Office might reject your petition if the first actual question does not at least begin on the first page.

Questions Presented Form

The Questions Presented can either be true questions, ending with a question mark, or whether-statements, like, "Whether the lower court..."

Parties Listing Rule

Rule 14.1(b)(i)

Parties Listing: ATTENTION

If you did not list all petitioners and all respondents in the caption on the cover, you must include a parties listing in the index (Rule 14.1(b)(i)).

Parties Listing Tip

The Parties Listing page can be used to explain inconsistencies between a lower court caption and the caption of your petition. If a party has been substituted for another since the lower court proceedings (i.e., a new governor took office or a new warden appointed), if the parties were aligned differently below, or if you dropped a party because you believe that party has no interest in the outcome of the petition (see Rule 12.6), you can describe those circumstances in your Parties Listing.

Corporate Disclosure Rules

Rule 14.1(b)(ii)   &   Rule 29.6

Corporate Disclosure Plain Language

If any petitioner is a non-governmental corporation, this is where you must make the disclosures required by Rule 29.6.

Corporate Disclosure Form

Many filers opt to combine the Parties Listing and Corporate Disclosure into a single section, with a heading that reads something like, "Parties to the Proceedings and Rule 29.6 Statement."

Corporate Disclosure Tip

If a petitioner’s identification on the cover or in the Parties Listing does not clearly demonstrate that the party is a NOT a non-governmental corporation, you can use the Parties Listing or the Corporate Disclosure to clear up any ambiguity, something like, "XYZ, Ltd. is a sole proprietorship and not subject to disclosure under Rule 29.6." This will help the Clerk's Office determine that you have satisfied the Rules, and avoid possible docketing delays.

Statement of Related Cases Rule

Rule 14.1(b)(iii)

Statement of Related Cases Plain Language

This is a required section for virtually all petitions, because the term “related cases” includes the lower court proceedings in this case. If applicable, you should also list cases arising out of the same occurrence or circumstances, raising similar legal questions, but that were addressed in separate legal proceedings.

Statement of Related Cases Guidance

The Court offers an example of a Statement of Related Cases listing in its guide Guide to Filing Paid Cases.

Statement of Related Cases Form

List each separate proceeding by identifying the court, the docket number and case caption in that court, and the judgment date.

Statement of Related Cases Tip

The information provided here is similar to the Opinions Below requirement in your main brief (Rule 14.1(d)), but you must include both sections.

Table of Contents Rules

Rule 14.1(c)  and   Rule 34.2

Table of Contents Plain Language

Every document with more than 1,500 countable words must have a listing of the different brief sections, including an indication of the page where each listed section begins.

Table of Contents Form

Most filers list all sections and subsections of the Index and the Brief, but not listing the Table of Contents section itself, or not listing any Index section are acceptable style choices.

Table of Contents Tip

Changes in the main Brief will very often require changes in the Table of Contents. We recommend that you completely finalize your Brief before completing the Table of Contents. If you do make any late Brief changes, you will also need to re-check the Table of Contents. Even a minor change can cause page breaks to shift, requiring a Table of Contents update.

Table of Contents Tip

When drafting your Table of Contents, consider cutting and pasting section headings directly from the source page to ensure that the Table of Contents entry exactly matches the referenced heading.

Appendix Table of Contents Rules

Rule 14.1(c)   and   Rule 34.4

Appendix Table of Contents Plain Language

Every document filed with Appendix material must have a listing of the different Appendix documents, including an indication of the page where each Appendix document begins.

Appendix Table of Contents: ATTENTION

While the Rules generally excuse documents with 1,500 or fewer words from including a regular Table of Contents, the Appendix Table of Contents Rule 34.4 does not offer that exemption; all documents of any size that have Appendix material attached must include an Appendix Table of Contents.

Appendix Table of Contents Form

In a Petition, the Appendix Table of Contents must appear at the end of the regular Table of Contents (Rule 14.1(c)) - often with a designation like, “APPENDIX” followed by the individual listings - and must provide “a description of each document in the appendix” (Rule 34.4). The description should provide at least enough information to allow the reader to distinguish one document from another. Listings for court orders typically include the court name, document title, and date of entry. Each listing must also indicate the page where the document begins.

Appendix Table of Contents Tip

Many filers opt to include an Appendix Table of Contents between the end of the Brief, at the beginning of the Appendix material. However, Rule 14.1(c) still requires that a Petition also include an Appendix Table of Contents as a part of the regular Table of Contents.

Table of Authorities Rules

Rule 14.1(c)  and  Rule 34.2

Table of Authorities Plain Language

Every document with more than 1,500 countable words must have a listing of authorities cited in the main Brief, including an indication of every page where each listed authority can be found.

Table of Authorities Form

Your Table of Authorities should be broken into subsections by type of authority, in this sequence: cases, constitutional provisions, statutes, treatises, and other materials (if the Brief does not contain any authorities of one of these types, the subsection can be omitted). Within each subsection, the listing should be arranged in alpha-numeric sequence. Rule 34.2.

Table of Authorities Tip

Often a particular authority might be cited numerous times throughout the Brief, so much so that listing each page reference would create a cluttered-looking Table of Authorities, and would be ultimately unhelpful to your reader. A very common convention for authorities cited on more than five different pages is to replace the page listings with an italicized, "passim." From the Latin word for “scattered” this is a perfectly acceptable method of indicating that the authority is well-used throughout.

Table of Authorities Tip

Inexperienced filers will sometimes treat the Table of Authorities like the Constitutional And Statutory Provisions Involved section (Rule 14.1(f)), but each of these elements serves a separate purpose, and both are required in your Petition. The Table of Authorities appears in the Index and is intended to be a reference guide to help your reader to locate different authorities within the Brief. The Constitutional And Statutory Provisions Involved section appears in the main Brief, and is intended to help the Court identify the core constitutional or statutory provision that lies at the heart of the controversy.
